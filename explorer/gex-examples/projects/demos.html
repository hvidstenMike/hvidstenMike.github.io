Micro-World Examples

Golden Ratio:  Periodically in J-term I teach a course called "Geometry and the Imagination" for general education credit.  One of the activities that I have students do is to discover the golden ratio.  We do this by first scanning in photos of each student.  Then, we create a "ruler" to measure ratios of the face. Since the eyes are such a central component of the face, we compute the ratio of the distance from the eyebrows to the bottom of the chin over the distance from the top of the head to the eyebrows.  We average these ratios over all the students and find a number somewhere around 1.6.

Then, we experiment with sub-dividing a segment and computing various ratios of the sub-segments. One of these is the ratio of the entire segment to a longer sub-segment and another is the ratio of the longer sub-segment to the shorter.  Students are then asked to find where these two ratios match up. Surprisingly, at least to the students, they again come up with the ratio of about 1.6.  A homework assignment is to find as many instances of this ratio as they can in nature, buildings, appliances, etc.

Holonomy and Curved Space: In the upper-level Geometry course that I teach I have the students open up a Hyperbolic micro-world in Geometry Explorer and then observe what happens to figures as they are moved around in that world.  First, I have them take a figure and move it in a broad loop near the boundary of the Poincare disk.  Then, they are asked to move the figure in a very small loop.  From this experience, students gain a very intuitive idea of what it means for a space to be "curved" and yet still "locally Euclidean".  After doing this lab we move on to mathematically define holonomy, parallel transport, curvature and finally talk about the Gauss-Bonnet Formula. Students have a much better appreciation for what these results mean after seeing the "physical" manifestations of these in the hyperbolic micro-world.

Turtle Geometry and L-Systems:  Geometry courses are often somewhat "sterile" in that there is not much discussion of the geometry of the natural world.  As Benoit Mandelbrot was fond of saying "Montains are not cones, clouds are not spheres, trees are not cylinders".  I end the upper-level Geometry course with a unit on fractals and in particular on Lindenmayer Systems (or L-Systems).  These are grammar re-writing systems that can be used to model the branching and growth parameters of plants. In a lab on L-systems students learn about turtle geometry and then about how grammars can be used to encapsulate the "blueprints" of plants.
